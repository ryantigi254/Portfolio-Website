Projects

  • Building a reproducible benchmark to stress-test mental-health LLMs across three alignment failure modes.

    This CSY3055 Natural Language Processing project develops a compact, reproducible evaluation harness that measures clinical reasoning reliability without retrieval in large language models destined for mental-health support tooling. The work addresses a critical gap: aligned assistants can appear helpful while exhibiting three failure modes that matter clinically: unfaithful reasoning (plausible explanations that don't match correct diagnostic logic), sycophancy under social pressure which harms truthfulness, and longitudinal drift where guidance becomes inconsistent across sessions.

    The benchmark evaluates three models: PsyLLM (a fine-tuned counselling-oriented model), Qwen/Qwen3-8B (untuned baseline), and openai/gpt-oss-20b (larger generic reasoning comparator). The project includes three focused studies testing faithfulness of reasoning traces, empathy–truth trade-offs under social pressure, and longitudinal therapeutic continuity across sessions.

    Inspired by cutting-edge AI safety research from groups like Anthropic and Apollo Research, this work directly addresses alignment safety concerns in mental-health applications. The deliverable includes a public benchmark repository with evaluation metrics, visualisations, and a failure taxonomy that supports rigorous, domain-specific checks for counselling agents.

    Developing a reproducible benchmark to evaluate clinical reasoning reliability in mental-health LLMs. Testing three models (PsyLLM, Qwen, GPT-OSS) across three alignment failure modes: unfaithful reasoning, sycophancy under pressure, and longitudinal drift. Inspired by frontier AI safety research on in-context scheming and alignment faking.
